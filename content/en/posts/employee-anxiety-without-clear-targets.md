In the previous article, we discussed how unclear organizational goals can trap teams in an "efficiency paradox"—everyone is busy bustling around, yet no one knows where they're headed.

Today, I want to shift the focus to the individual level and explore what employees truly experience when goals are ambiguous. You'll find that this anxiety is not simply pressure; it's a persistent drain and unease that comes from sprinting at full speed through a fog.

The most immediate feeling is a strong sense of loss of control. When you work hard to complete tasks at hand but can't see how they connect to the ultimate value, every acceleration feels like running in place.

Psychologically, this state can easily lead to "learned helplessness"—no matter how hard you try, the outcome seems unaffected.

A more subtle experience is the erosion of your inner judgment. You start to question: Does what I'm doing really matter? Am I truly helping the organization move forward, or am I just creating a facade of busyness?

Vague goals also quietly drain our attention and cognitive resources. You may find yourself spending more time pondering "What does my boss actually want?" or "Is this meaningful?" than actually doing the work. It's like a high-intensity mental weight-training session—energy is depleted, but no progress is made. Even after clocking out, your mind remains trapped in a state of turmoil, feeling exhausted yet achieving nothing. This invisible "cognitive tax" is more draining than overtime itself.

What's even more mentally exhausting is a misalignment of values. When goals conflict with your personal beliefs, anxiety escalates to another level. You might value long-term impact but are forced to chase short-term metrics; you champion collaboration but find yourself in a hyper-competitive culture. This dissonance doesn't just sap efficiency—it makes you question your professional identity: Am I becoming someone I don't want to be? This kind of anxiety is incomparable to ordinary stress.

So, what can employees do in an environment with vague goals?

A good starting point is to proactively seek a "sense of direction." First, learn to be your own "goal translator." Instead of mechanically executing tasks, ask "why" when you receive an assignment. Understand the intent and value behind it, so you can channel your energy into what truly matters.

Next, establish a "personal goal ruler" for yourself. Use it to evaluate each task's contribution to the organization and its value to your own growth. For tasks that score low, don't strive for perfection—just do enough to get by, and save your energy for more important work.

One more thing is crucial: learn to manage your attention, rather than passively receiving tasks. When a new task comes in, don't accept it unconditionally. Instead, bring a proposed plan to negotiate priorities, directing your limited resources toward truly valuable directions.

Slowing down is not procrastination; it's about giving efficiency real meaning. Efficiency without direction is just noise that accelerates. When the direction is clear, even a slower pace is more stable and powerful than blind sprinting.

Ultimately, clear goals are not just a tool for efficiency—they are a psychological asset. They provide direction in a complex workplace, reduce internal friction, and safeguard the sense of meaning in our work.
