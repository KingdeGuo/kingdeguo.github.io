# The Company Can Run Without Anyone, So Why Is Asking for Time Off So Hard?

There's a saying in the workplace that sounds reassuring: "The company can run without anyone." But when it's your turn to ask for leave, the vibe shifts instantly, and your boss's expression becomes harder to read than the weather forecast. You think to yourself: "Didn't they say the company can run without anyone? Why does it get so complicated when it's my turn?" — The truth is, there's a whole set of "workplace physics" hiding beneath the surface.

Let's start with the first paradox: Asking for leave is like booking a train ticket — the closer it gets, the more uncertain it becomes. When things are calm, you say, "I'd like to take two days off next week," and suddenly client reviews, system launches, and leadership presentations all pile up at once. You think it's a coincidence? Actually, it's the "Law of Leave": the moment you plan to rest always collides with everyone else's most stressful time.

The second phenomenon: Handovers are never as smooth as you imagine. You write a handover document, thinking you've covered everything, but when your colleague opens it, it feels like they're playing a "puzzle game" — key steps are buried in comments, and the logic is anyone's guess. So the boss does the math: rather than letting someone else fumble through the dark, it's safer to keep you at the helm, at least to avoid a major disaster.

Then there's the chain of responsibility. When you want time off, your boss has to decide who covers for you. Unfortunately, "covering" in the workplace basically means "getting dragged into the mess." Everyone is already as busy as a delivery driver juggling a stack of orders, and the moment you step away, the team's rhythm falls apart.

There's also a hidden dynamic: Asking for leave is essentially a "trust test." If you hand over clearly and leave no mess behind, your boss will be more relaxed next time. But if you left a trail of chaos after your last break, their first reaction when you ask again will likely be — "Denied. Play it safe."

Finally, there's a subtle psychological factor: fairness. When you're gone, someone else has to pick up the slack, and after a few times, they can't help but think, "Why does it always have to be me?" The boss also worries that approving leave too casually might breed collective resentment. So the safest move is to stall — stall until you drop the idea yourself.

So, at the end of the day, asking for leave is hard not because you can't be replaced, but because no one *wants* to replace you. The boss fears a disrupted rhythm, colleagues fear extra blame, and you fear losing your plane ticket. Everyone is afraid, and things get stuck.

But life goes on, and you will need time off. The trick is simple: give plenty of notice, hand over cleanly, and bring back a little "gesture" when you return. When snacks suddenly appear in the break room, everyone's mood lifts, and the burden doesn't feel so heavy.

One last thought: The company can run without anyone, but the difficulty of taking leave has never been about "whether it's possible" — it's about "whether people are willing."
